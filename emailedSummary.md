

**CPS-CIR: A Closed-Loop Cyber-Physical System for Flood Duration Estimation, Pre-Code Building Vulnerability, and Community Retrofit Decision Support**

**Program**: NSF Future CoRe – CPS-CIR Track (NSF 25-543) | **PIs**: Napolitano (lead, structural vulnerability and community co-design), Wauthier (SAR processing and uncertainty quantification), Busse (hydrological modeling and mass balance)

**Overview**: The small, aging communities most repeatedly devastated by riverine flooding are the least equipped to use existing flood damage assessment frameworks, which require hydrodynamic simulation pipelines demanding data, software, and expertise far beyond what small municipalities can sustain. Emergency managers, historic preservation officials, and building owners in many towns like these lack any engineering framework that translates a flood event into actionable, building-specific guidance on where limited retrofit dollars belong. Our community partners in Montpelier, VT and Marshall, NC, two small, aging riverine communities have specifically identified inundation duration, not just peak flood stage, as a decision-relevant variable which motivates every modeling constraint in this proposal.

We propose a closed-loop cyber-physical system with four coupled layers. The **physical layer** is the flood-building interaction system. Pre-code construction, load-bearing masonry and timber structures built before modern building codes, is ubiquitous across small historic riverine communities yet absent from every existing vulnerability framework; it degrades under extended inundation through duration-governed mechanisms that current assessment tools neither observe nor model. The **sensing layer** is multi-temporal SAR and optical imagery: Sentinel-1 C-band and Capella X-band coherence and amplitude time series that track the physical state of flooded building surfaces through the recovery period. The **cyber layer** is a Bayesian data fusion framework that integrates SAR coherence persistence with community-scale hydrological mass balance, the only two data streams accessible without a full hydrodynamic model, into probabilistic inundation duration estimates with propagated uncertainty. The **decision layer** is a retrofit allocation framework, co-designed with community stakeholders, that maps probabilistic duration outputs to building-specific damage state probabilities, distinguishes community-scale drainage interventions from individual building hardening, and closes the loop as subsequent SAR acquisitions over retrofitted structures validate outcome predictions and update model priors. This closed loop, satellite sensing to probabilistic inference to community decision to physical change, is the defining CPS contribution, distinguishing this project from prior approaches that terminate at model output.

Community partners in Montpelier and Marshall participate as co-designers throughout, shaping model architecture in Year 1, validating framework outputs against locally held knowledge in Year 2, and stress-testing decision tools for independent use in Year 3. Two independent flood events, Montpelier 2023 and Marshall 2024, provide model validation across heterogeneous community contexts, from moderate institutional capacity (Montpelier) to minimal infrastructure and high poverty (Marshall, under 1,000 residents); the contrast isolates which framework components transfer across community contexts and which require local calibration, establishing the scientific basis for generalization. The aging pre-code building stock defining these historic cores lines the riverbanks of thousands of small towns across Appalachia, New England, and the rural South, communities disproportionately low-income and without any engineering framework for flood retrofit decision-making.

**Intellectual Merit**: This project advances CPS science in two foundational ways: (1) establishing that community-accessible sensing modalities, fused under a Bayesian probabilistic machine learning architecture, can substitute for resource-intensive physics-based simulation intermediaries in closed-loop CPS decision systems, a generalizable principle for infrastructure contexts that cannot support full hydrodynamic modeling; and (2) advancing a community co-design methodology that treats locally held knowledge as structural input to model validation, contributing to CPS practice for socio-technical systems where stakeholder knowledge cannot be separated from system calibration.

**RO1: Develop and validate a Bayesian cyber-physical fusion framework for probabilistic inundation duration estimation.**

The core CPS contribution is architecting a tight coupling between two heterogeneous sensing streams, SAR coherence persistence and hydrological mass balance, and a physical process (flood duration) that neither stream directly measures. Duration governs cumulative damage in pre-code buildings and is the enabling input for the RO2 decision framework. The Bayesian fusion framework treats mass balance as an informative prior on duration and SAR coherence decay as the observational likelihood, coherence declines as flood-induced moisture and structural deformation alter building surfaces, and its recovery rate is the observable proxy for inundation duration, yielding a posterior duration distribution that propagates uncertainty through to the decision layer. Uncertainty varying with orbit geometry, revisit interval, and drainage basin geometry is characterized and propagated rather than collapsed. This architecture has not been attempted; prior remote sensing uses SAR to calibrate hydrodynamic models, not replace their duration output. Preliminary Sentinel-1 coherence analysis of Montpelier 2023 across four polarization/orbit combinations shows consistent coherence depression concentrated in the downtown core persisting nearly two weeks post-peak, establishing the observational foundation. Capella SAR acquisition will be budgeted to constrain the recovery slope and reduce posterior uncertainty.

**RO2: Co-design and validate a retrofit decision allocation framework from the coupled duration-damage system.**

RO2 converts probabilistic duration estimates into building-specific damage state probabilities and actionable retrofit guidance, what-if simulations that let communities weigh community-scale drainage investments against individual building hardening. Napolitano develops a duration-based fragility surface for the pre-code load-bearing building archetype most prevalent in both study communities, extending existing component-based methodology with material parameters specific to older construction whose duration-dependent degradation mechanisms are physically distinct from the archetypes currently in community resilience portfolios. This fragility surface receives duration estimates from the cyber layer and produces the damage state probabilities that feed the decision framework. The team will contrasts results across both flood events to decompose damage attributable to community-scale drainage failure from building-specific material vulnerability, a decomposition that maps directly onto the retrofit decision. Napolitano will lead annual co-design workshops with emergency managers, historic preservation officials, and building owners in Montpelier and Marshall: Year 1 surfaces locally held knowledge to constrain prior distributions; Year 2 validates framework outputs against community-observed flood behavior; Year 3 stress-tests the decision tools for independent use and probes transferability conditions, drainage basin geometry, building density, and institutional capacity, for other riverine towns.

**Broader Impacts**: The closed-loop CPS framework transforms how flood-affected communities interact with infrastructure sensing, enabling duration-based damage assessment where no path currently exists, deriving probabilistic duration from satellite observations and closing the loop as subsequent flood events validate and refine the model. The decision layer and co-design process are designed for communities without ongoing research support. Structured co-design with Montpelier and Marshall will validate the framework against real community decisions and will leave both communities with lasting independent capacity. The retrofit allocation framework distinguishes community drainage interventions from individual building hardening, a resource allocation problem every historic riverine downtown faces with no existing engineering guidance, and most consequential for the disproportionately low-income, repeatedly flood-affected communities this project serves. Graduate students will receive cross-disciplinary training in SAR remote sensing, probabilistic structural modeling, and participatory community research. The CIR co-design methodology, documented as a transferable protocol, advances CPS practice for connected communities, establishing how resident knowledge anchors model validation and community decisions feed back into physical infrastructure sensing across networks of similarly situated towns.
