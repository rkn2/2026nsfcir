\textbf{Project goals:}
Small, repeatedly-flooded municipalities with pre-code building stock have no
engineering tool to evaluate whether community-scale drainage investment or distributed
building hardening better reduces flood damage.
We propose a closed-loop cyber-physical system (CPS) organized around four coupled layers:
the \textbf{physical layer} (pre-code load-bearing masonry and timber structures, absent from existing vulnerability frameworks);
the \textbf{sensing layer} (Sentinel-1 and Capella SAR coherence time series, with ICEYE providing building-level inundation duration as a validation reference);
the \textbf{cyber layer} (Bayesian fusion of SAR coherence with hydrological mass balance and community-elicited priors on inundation duration); and
the \textbf{decision layer} (a multi-criteria retrofit allocation framework co-developed with Montpelier, VT community partners).
\textbf{RQ1} (Napolitano, Wauthier, Busse): Can this CPS provide a decision-adequate pathway to probabilistic flood damage assessment for small historic municipalities without resource-intensive simulation?
\textbf{RQ2} (Napolitano with all PIs): Under what conditions does locally held community knowledge, operationalized as informative Bayesian priors, produce measurably better-calibrated damage assessments than data-only approaches?
The defining contribution is making community knowledge mathematically load-bearing in the Bayesian inference layer rather than supplementary to it.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Status quo}
Small, aging communities at the confluence of riverine flood hazard and pre-code building stock face a resilience challenge that no existing engineering framework is designed to address. 
Thousands of towns across the northeastern United States and Appalachian corridor share this profile: riverine flood exposure with multi-day inundation durations, dense historic cores of load-bearing masonry and timber construction built before modern building codes, and institutional capacity below the threshold required for hydrodynamic simulation \cite{placeholder_nfip_acs}.
FEMA's National Flood Insurance Program enrolls over 22,000 communities nationwide; the subset in this region with significant pre-1940 building stock, without sustained in-house hydraulic modeling capacity, and with a history of riverine flooding numbers in the thousands. 
For these communities, every flood event surfaces the same unresolved question: where do limited retrofit dollars belong?

That question involves two distinct decisions that existing frameworks cannot separate. 
The first is the individual building question: which buildings sustain damage severe enough to justify investment in flood barriers, foundation waterproofing, or structural hardening? 
The second is the community-scale question: would a drainage investment such as a retention basin, channel improvement, or permeable infrastructure upgrade reduce building damage enough across the community to justify its cost relative to distributed individual retrofits? 
Answering the second question requires a model that connects upstream drainage decisions to downstream building damage probabilities across the community's building stock.
Without it, municipalities cannot make the case to funders, to state partners, or to their own residents that community-scale investment is warranted. No such model exists for small communities with pre-code building stock, and no existing framework allows these two decisions to be compared on the same probabilistic basis.

Existing flood damage assessment tools were designed for the resources and building stocks of larger municipalities. Hydrodynamic simulation pipelines such as HEC-RAS and LISFLOOD-FP require calibrated hydraulic models, high-resolution topographic data, and sustained technical capacity that small towns cannot maintain. Damage functions in HAZUS and FEMA P-58 are built around post-1945 construction archetypes; pre-code load-bearing masonry and timber structures, the dominant building type in repeatedly-flooded historic downtowns, are absent from existing fragility frameworks. Emergency managers, historic preservation officials, and building owners in these communities have no engineering tool that translates a flood event into building-specific guidance on where limited retrofit dollars belong.

Montpelier, VT exemplifies this challenge in its sharpest form. It is a small, aging riverine community with a dense historic core of pre-code load-bearing masonry, limited institutional capacity relative to hydrodynamic simulation requirements, and a history of repeated flood exposure spanning more than a century. Montpelier experienced catastrophic flooding in July 2023 that damaged 80\% of its National Register Historic District, and sustained significant flood damage again in 2024; the 2023 and 2024 events together provide the empirical foundation for this project.
Community partners have learned through repeated flooding that how long water remained mattered as much as how high it rose, and that no existing framework helps them evaluate whether that distinction favors community-scale drainage investments over distributed building retrofits.

These buildings degrade under extended inundation through duration-governed mechanisms including mortar softening, masonry unit saturation, and rising damp-driven cohesion loss that current assessment tools neither observe nor model. 
Existing community flood resilience frameworks are predominantly top-down in design, built around standardized data collection and technical modeling pipelines that low-capacity localities with limited government resources and technical expertise cannot sustain. 
Community-generated data and locally held knowledge offer an underutilized pathway to resilience assessment in resource-constrained settings, yet no existing framework operationalizes this knowledge as structural input to probabilistic assessments that inform both building-specific hardening decisions and community-scale drainage investment.

\subsection{Research questions and intellectual merit}

The intellectual merit of this work addresses two fundamental research questions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0pt]
\item \textbf{RQ1 (Napolitano, Wauthier, and Busse):} Can a closed-loop CPS that couples community-accessible satellite sensing with Bayesian inference provide a decision-adequate pathway to probabilistic flood damage assessment for small historic municipalities, enabling them to evaluate both building-specific hardening and community-scale drainage investments without resource-intensive simulation?
\item \textbf{RQ2 (Napolitano with all PIs):} When locally held community knowledge
is operationalized as informative Bayesian priors and empirical damage observations,
under what conditions does it produce measurably more accurate and better-calibrated
damage assessments than data-only approaches, and what does the pattern of improvement
reveal about the epistemic role of co-design in CPS calibration for socio-technical
systems?
\end{itemize}

These questions together span three scientific contributions.
First, this research establishes whether a closed-loop CPS coupling community-accessible satellite sensing with Bayesian inference can provide decision-adequate probabilistic flood damage assessment for retrofit allocation purposes without hydrodynamic simulation, advancing a generalizable CPS architecture principle: that community-accessible sensing, fused under a Bayesian framework, is sufficient for the specific investment decisions small municipalities face when resource-intensive simulation is not an option.
Second, it characterizes the conditions under which locally held community knowledge, operationalized as informative Bayesian priors and damage observation training data, improves CPS model accuracy and calibration relative to data-only approaches, and what the pattern of improvement reveals about the epistemic role of co-design in CPS development: specifically, whether community knowledge functions as a structural calibration input or whether its contribution is primarily social.
Third, it establishes a principle about CPS physical layer design: when the cyber layer produces duration estimates rather than flood depth, the physical model must take duration as its governing input rather than just the depth variable that existing fragility frameworks assume. This research demonstrates that principle by producing the first empirically-derived, duration-dependent fragility surfaces for pre-code load-bearing masonry archetypes from field damage observations across two flood events.
Montpelier's 2023 and 2024 flood events provide a within-community train-test structure: the inference chain and fragility parameters are calibrated on 2023 observations and validated against 2024 data, which were collected independently and span a different storm magnitude and antecedent conditions. This design directly tests whether the framework generalizes across event characteristics within the same community, without requiring a second study site.
RQ1 is addressed through RO1; RQ2 is addressed through RO2.

\section{Current state-of-the-art}

Each of the three technical domains underpinning this proposal has advanced substantially as a standalone field: SAR remote sensing for flood characterization, hydrodynamic and hydrological modeling for flood dynamics, and fragility-based vulnerability assessment for building damage estimation. 
Yet none has been integrated with the others into a system accessible to small, resource-constrained municipalities, and each contains a specific gap that prevents it from serving the building stock and community context where flood damage is most persistent and least addressed. 
SAR methods characterize inundation extent but not duration; hydrodynamic models estimate flood dynamics accurately but require technical capacity that small municipalities cannot sustain; and existing fragility frameworks built around modern construction archetypes do not capture the duration-governed degradation mechanisms of the pre-code load-bearing building stock dominant in repeatedly-flooded historic communities.

\subsection{Gap summary}
These gaps (Tab.~\ref{tab:research_summary}) together define a decision problem that no existing framework can support. 
Small municipalities with pre-code building stock face two investment decisions simultaneously: which individual buildings warrant hardening, and whether community-scale drainage improvements reduce building damage enough across the stock to justify their cost over distributed individual retrofits. 
Answering either question on the same probabilistic basis requires connecting upstream drainage decisions to downstream building damage probabilities through a physical model of the relevant building stock. 
The CPS proposed here closes that connection by coupling community-accessible SAR sensing with hydrological mass balance in a Bayesian framework, linking those outputs to building damage state through empirically-derived duration-dependent fragility surfaces for pre-code archetypes, and co-developing with community partners a multi-criteria retrofit allocation framework that places both investment decisions on the same probabilistic footing. 
A parallel gap motivates the CIR contribution: locally held community knowledge enters the system as structural calibration input rather than post-hoc validation, directly testing whether co-design improves the scientific outputs of the CPS rather than only its social reception.

\subsection{Preliminary results: ICEYE building-level duration data, Montpelier 2023 and 2024}

ICEYE commercial SAR provides building-level flood depth and inundation duration at
6-hour temporal resolution for both Montpelier flood events, covering the full inundation
and recovery period in each case.
For the 2023 event, ICEYE data spans the catastrophic July flood that damaged 80\% of the
National Register Historic District, providing depth and duration measurements at
building scale across the historic downtown pre-code masonry core.
For the 2024 event, comparable building-level temporal coverage is available for the
same downtown structures.
ICEYE data was acquired through a data access agreement with ICEYE; the project budget
includes a planned data purchase of approximately \$5,000.

These two datasets together enable a rigorous train-test validation structure that was not
previously possible.
The 2023 event serves as the calibration dataset: building-level Sentinel-1 coherence
recovery rates from Subtask~1.1 are paired with ICEYE-measured inundation duration per
building to fit the coherence recovery parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$,
and ICEYE-measured duration and depth are used alongside field damage observations to
calibrate the fragility surface parameters.
The 2024 event is the held-out test: the fitted inference chain and fragility surface,
with no adjustment, are applied to 2024 SAR coherence data and evaluated against
independently collected ICEYE duration measurements and 2024 field damage records.
Because the two events differ in storm magnitude, antecedent conditions, and inundation
pattern, this within-community test directly evaluates framework generalizability across
event characteristics.
ICEYE also provides both inundation duration and peak depth at building scale for the
same structures where field damage state is recorded, enabling the multivariate fragility
regression to directly test whether duration adds explanatory power beyond depth without
relying on indirect proxies for either variable.

RO1 builds and validates the technical CPS by coupling four components: SAR coherence time series (Subtask~1.1), hydrological mass balance (Subtask~1.2), a Bayesian fusion framework that integrates these two sensing streams into probabilistic duration estimates (Subtask~1.3), and duration-dependent fragility surfaces that connect those duration estimates to building-specific damage state probabilities (Subtask~1.4).
The Bayesian fusion framework treats mass balance as an informative prior on duration and SAR coherence decay as the observational likelihood: coherence declines as flood-induced moisture and structural deformation alter building surfaces, and its recovery rate is the observable proxy for inundation duration.
The resulting posterior duration distribution propagates uncertainty through to the decision layer.
Uncertainty varying with orbit geometry, revisit interval, and drainage basin geometry is characterized and propagated rather than collapsed.
The fragility surface development in Subtask~1.4 provides the physical model layer of the CPS, connecting the Bayesian duration output to the damage states the RO2 decision framework requires.
Subtask~1.5 validates the full RO1 system against observed flood behavior in Montpelier, using the 2024 event as a held-out test.

\textbf{Subtask 1.2: Hydrological Mass Balance Model (lead: Busse).}
PI Busse develops a hydrological mass balance model for the Winooski watershed above Montpelier, estimating the drainage timescale $\hat{\tau}_k$ for each building-scale sub-basin as a function of contributing area, effective impervious fraction, antecedent soil moisture, and storm precipitation, constrained by USGS Winooski gauge records (station 04288000).
The drainage timescale $\hat{\tau}_k$ represents the expected duration of standing water under gravitational drainage and serves as the physically grounded basis for the Bayesian prior on inundation duration.
Inundation duration at building $i$ located in sub-basin $k$ is modeled as:
\begin{equation}
  T_i \sim \operatorname{LogNormal}(\mu_i,\, \sigma^2)
\end{equation}
where $\mu_i = \log \hat{\tau}_k + \delta_i^{\mathrm{comm}}$ is the log-scale prior mean, $\hat{\tau}_k$ is the mass balance drainage timescale for sub-basin $k$, $\delta_i^{\mathrm{comm}}$ is a building-level adjustment elicited from community knowledge in Subtask~2.1 (knowledge of specific drainage obstructions, historically slow drainage routes, or anomalous building conditions), and $\sigma^2$ captures residual local drainage uncertainty quantified from gauge-record variability across historical Winooski flood events.
The LogNormal family is appropriate because duration is strictly positive and right-skewed, and because log-scale reasoning is consistent with the power-law coherence recovery model in Subtask~1.3.

\textbf{Subtask 1.3: Bayesian Fusion Framework (leads: Napolitano, Wauthier, and Busse).}
The Bayesian fusion framework integrates the hydrological prior from Subtask~1.2 with the SAR coherence time series from Subtask~1.1 to produce a posterior distribution over inundation duration at each building.
Let $t = 0$ denote flood onset; SAR acquisition $k$ occurs at time $t_k$ measured from that origin, and $T_i$ is the inundation duration at building $i$, also measured from flood onset.
Coherence is depressed while the building is inundated and recovers exponentially after the water drains:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{C}(t_k,\, T_i) \;=\; C_\infty - (C_\infty - C_{\mathrm{flood}})\, e^{-(t_k - T_i)/\tau_C(T_i)}, \quad t_k > T_i
\end{equation}
where $C_\infty$ is the pre-flood coherence baseline, $C_{\mathrm{flood}}$ is the minimum coherence during inundation, and $\tau_C(T_i) = \alpha T_i^\beta$ is the coherence recovery timescale as a power function of inundation duration.
The power-law form allows the recovery rate to scale nonlinearly with duration; $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are calibrated from the Montpelier 2023 scatter of Sentinel-1 coherence recovery rate versus ICEYE-measured building-level duration, constituting the primary calibration result of the preliminary work.
Observed coherence at each acquisition is modeled as Gaussian about this expectation:
\begin{equation}
  C_{i,k} \mid T_i \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\hat{C}(t_k,\, T_i),\; \sigma_C^2\right)
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_C^2$ aggregates speckle noise and orbit-geometry uncertainty, estimated from stable-surface coherence over non-flooded reference structures in each scene.
Conditioning on all $K$ SAR acquisitions spanning the inundation and recovery window, the posterior duration distribution for building $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
  p(T_i \mid C_{i,1}, \ldots, C_{i,K}) \;\propto\; p(T_i) \prod_{k=1}^{K} p(C_{i,k} \mid T_i)
\end{equation}
where $p(T_i)$ is the LogNormal prior from Equation~(1).
Because $T_i$ is one-dimensional and the likelihood is smooth, the posterior is computed on a dense grid rather than by sampling, propagating full uncertainty from both observational streams into the damage and decision layers without collapsing to a point estimate.
The 2024 Montpelier event is the held-out test: parameters $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\sigma_C^2$ fitted to 2023 data are applied without adjustment to 2024 SAR coherence and compared against independently collected ICEYE 2024 duration measurements.

\textbf{Subtask 1.4: Duration-Dependent Fragility Surface Development (lead: Napolitano).}
Duration and peak flood stage are correlated in observational data, so the fragility surfaces are developed using a two-component approach that separates the duration effect physically before fitting it statistically.
The \textit{physical component} grounds the duration claim in material science: component-based analysis of duration-governed degradation mechanisms in pre-code load-bearing masonry (mortar softening rate, brick saturation curves, rising damp penetration depth as a function of inundation time) provides a theoretically grounded basis for why duration adds explanatory power beyond depth, establishing the functional form of the duration effect independently of observational confounding.
The \textit{empirical component} calibrates the fragility surface parameters from field damage observations from the 2023 Montpelier flood and validates them against the 2024 event, with peak depth treated as a covariate and duration entering as the primary variable whose additional explanatory power is directly tested.
ICEYE building-level data, acquired at 6-hour temporal resolution for both events, provides both the duration measurement and the depth covariate directly at building scale, enabling the multivariate regression without relying on indirect proxies for either variable.

The fragility surface for damage state $ds$ takes the form:
\begin{equation}
  P(DS \geq ds \mid T_i,\, d_i) \;=\; \Phi\!\left(\frac{\ln T_i - \lambda_{ds}(d_i)}{\zeta_{ds}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $T_i$ is inundation duration (hours), $d_i$ is peak inundation depth (m), $\lambda_{ds}(d_i) = \lambda_0 + \gamma \ln d_i$ is the log-scale median duration capacity (depth shifts the threshold at which duration governs damage), $\zeta_{ds}$ is the log-standard deviation of capacity, and $\Phi$ is the standard normal cumulative distribution function.
The physical component provides initial estimates of $\lambda_0$, $\gamma$, and $\zeta_{ds}$ from material science; the empirical component refines these parameters using the 2023 Montpelier field observations, and the fitted surface is applied unchanged to the 2024 event for validation.
The claim under test is that duration adds statistically significant explanatory power beyond depth alone, evaluated by comparing the bivariate model against a depth-only baseline using damage prediction accuracy across both events.
The posterior duration distribution $p(T_i \mid \mathbf{C})$ from Subtask~1.3 propagates through Equation~(5) by Monte Carlo integration, producing a building-specific damage state probability distribution that carries the full inferential uncertainty of the SAR and hydrological observation streams into the decision layer.

\textbf{Subtask 2.1: Community Co-Development Workshops and Knowledge Elicitation (lead: Napolitano).}
PI Napolitano will lead annual co-development workshops with emergency managers, historic preservation officials, and building owners in Montpelier across three years.
Community partners include Vermont State Historic Preservation Office representatives, Department of the Interior cultural heritage representatives, and the Montpelier planning commission; letters of collaboration from all community partners are included in the supplementary materials.
\textit{Year~1} surfaces locally held knowledge about which buildings held water longest, which drainage routes are historically slow, and which buildings have anomalous conditions; this knowledge is operationalized as informative Bayesian priors on inundation duration for the Subtask~1.3 fusion framework. Community partners also co-develop the criteria and weighting structure of the Subtask~2.3 retrofit allocation framework, shaping which investment outcomes matter most to their communities. Community-identified priorities are documented in structured form at the close of Year~1 workshops to enable assessment of priority incorporation in subsequent years.
\textit{Year~2} collects community-observed building damage reports from the 2023 and 2024 flood events, serving as empirical training data for the Subtask~1.4 fragility surfaces, and validates framework outputs against community-observed flood behavior. Community partners assess whether the framework outputs reflect the priorities they identified in Year~1; discrepancies are documented and used to update the framework architecture. \textit{Year~3} stress-tests the retrofit allocation framework for independent use, reassesses whether community priorities have shifted over the project period, and documents any framework adaptations required to maintain alignment with current community needs. \textbf{Subtask 2.3: Multi-Criteria Retrofit Allocation Framework (lead: Napolitano).}
The multi-criteria retrofit allocation framework translates the probabilistic RO1 outputs into decisions actionable by community partners in Montpelier without ongoing research support.
Community partners (Vermont SHPO representatives, Montpelier planning commission staff, and building owners) co-develop the criteria, weighting structure, and output format in Year~1 workshops, not merely validate a team-built tool.

For each retrofit action $a$ under consideration, the framework computes expected loss by integrating the action-modified damage probability over the Bayesian posterior duration distribution from Equation~(4):
\begin{equation}
  E[\text{loss} \mid a] \;=\; \int_0^\infty \sum_{i} P(DS_i \geq ds \mid T,\, d_i,\, a)\cdot L_i \cdot p(T \mid \mathbf{C},\, a)\, dT
\end{equation}
where $L_i$ is the replacement value at building $i$.
The action $a$ enters the integrand in two places depending on its type: a community-scale drainage investment modifies $p(T \mid \mathbf{C}, a)$ by reducing the expected inundation duration through the mass balance model; an individual building hardening action modifies $P(DS_i \geq ds \mid T, d_i, a)$ by raising the capacity threshold in Equation~(5).
Computing Equation~(6) under both action types and comparing the expected loss reduction against a no-action baseline places community-scale drainage investments and individual building retrofits on the same probabilistic basis, resolving the comparison that current frameworks cannot support.

Expected loss reduction is one criterion within the multi-criteria decision analysis score:
\begin{equation}
  S(a) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{J} w_j \cdot V_j(a)
\end{equation}
where criteria $V_j$ include structural risk reduction (from Equation~(6)), retrofit cost, historic preservation value, and community equity; weights $w_j$ are elicited from community partners in Year~1 workshops through structured pairwise comparison, formalized as the co-developed weighting structure that community partners own and can revisit.
The output interface presents $S(a)$ for each action alongside what-if simulations in which emergency managers and planning commission staff adjust weights and compare scenarios without any knowledge of SAR processing or Bayesian inference; all technical complexity is abstracted behind the decision-layer output.
Year~2 community assessment of whether outputs reflect Year~1 stated priorities drives any framework adaptations; Year~3 stress-tests independent use without researcher support.

\section{Broader impacts}

The closed-loop CPS framework transforms how flood-affected communities interact with infrastructure sensing, enabling the probabilistic flood damage assessments that small, resource-constrained municipalities need to evaluate both building-specific hardening and community-scale drainage investments, where no such pathway currently exists.
By enabling better-informed retrofit investment decisions, the framework directly improves the quality of life of residents who live, work, and rebuild in repeatedly-flooded historic communities, reducing the flood damage burden that falls disproportionately on towns with the fewest resources to recover.
The decision layer and co-development process are designed for communities without ongoing research support; the framework is demonstrated in Montpelier and documented for transfer to any municipality meeting the four scope conditions.
Emergency managers, historic preservation officials, and planning commission staff gain structured capacity to interpret probabilistic damage assessments and use satellite-informed decision tools independently, building the municipal technical literacy that currently does not exist in this community class.
Graduate students will receive cross-disciplinary training in SAR remote sensing, probabilistic structural modeling, and participatory community research.
The CIR co-design methodology, documented as a transferable protocol, advances CPS practice for connected communities by establishing how structured elicitation of locally held flood knowledge can be translated into informative Bayesian prior distributions, making resident knowledge mathematically load-bearing in model calibration rather than a post-hoc input, and how community retrofit decisions feed back into physical infrastructure sensing across networks of similarly situated towns.
